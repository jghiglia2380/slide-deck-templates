Market Research Guide

A step-by-step guide to investigating real markets and making smart purchasing decisions

Step 1: Identify Market Structure

Key Questions to Ask:

  • How many sellers are there? Count the actual competitors in your area or online
  • Can I easily switch between sellers? Are there switching costs or contracts?
  • Are the products identical or differentiated? Do sellers offer the same thing or unique features?
  • Can new competitors enter easily? What barriers exist (licenses, capital, technology)?

Decision Tree to Classification:

Q: How many sellers?
  • Many (50+): Likely Perfect Competition (e.g., farmers market, stock market)
  • Few (3-10): Likely Oligopoly (e.g., cell carriers, airlines)
  • One: Monopoly (e.g., local utility, patented drug)
Q: Products identical?
  • Yes: More competitive → Better for consumers
  • No: Differentiation → More pricing power for sellers
Q: Easy to switch?
  • Yes: High competition → Lower prices
  • No (contracts, switching costs): Less competition → Higher prices
Example: "I count 3 major wireless carriers in my area (Verizon, AT&T, T-Mobile). They offer similar but differentiated services (different coverage, plans, perks). I can switch, but there are some hassles (changing number, buying out phone). Conclusion: Oligopoly."
Step 2: Research Supply Conditions

What to Look For:

  • Inventory levels: Are sellers well-stocked or running low?
  • Production news: New factories opening? Closures? Supply chain issues?
  • Seasonal patterns: Does supply vary by time of year?
  • Competitive intensity: Are sellers aggressively marketing or holding back?

Where to Look:

  • Google Trends: See search interest over time (high interest = high demand)
  • Industry Reports: Search "[product] industry trends 2024"
  • Dealer Websites: "In stock" vs. "Limited availability" vs. "Backordered"
  • News Articles: "[product] shortage" or "[product] oversupply"
  • Inventory Counters: Some sites show exact stock numbers

Red Flags (Supply is Tight):

  • "Low inventory" or "Limited supply" messaging
  • "Order now" urgency tactics
  • Long wait times for delivery
  • Sellers removing discounts or promotions
  • News of production disruptions or material shortages
Example: "I visited 3 car dealerships. Two had 'limited inventory' signs and quoted 4-6 week wait times. News articles mention semiconductor shortage affecting production. Conclusion: Supply is constrained - prices likely elevated."
Step 3: Analyze Demand Patterns

Demand Factors to Research:

  • Seasonal cycles: When is demand highest vs. lowest for this product?
  • Economic conditions: Is the economy strong or weak? Does this affect demand?
  • Trends and fads: Is this product trendy right now or fading?
  • Demographic shifts: Are more or fewer people in the market for this?

Step 4: Find Price Data

Comparison Shopping Sites:

  • Google Shopping: Compare prices across sellers
  • Amazon: Check price history and "other sellers"
  • PriceGrabber, Shopzilla: Aggregators for many products
  • Cars: Kelley Blue Book (KBB), Edmunds, TrueCar
  • Electronics: Best Buy, B&H Photo, Newegg

Historical Price Trackers:

  • CamelCamelCamel: Amazon price history charts
  • Keepa: Price tracking with alerts
  • Google Shopping Insights: Price trends over time
  • Honey/Capital One Shopping: Price drop alerts

Step 5: Determine Timing

Decision Matrix: When to Buy

Supply Demand Recommendation Explanation
High Low BUY NOW ✓ Best buyer's market - abundant supply, few buyers
High High BUY SOON Balanced market, but supply may not last
Low Low WAIT if possible Low activity market, wait for supply to improve
Low High WAIT or pay premium Worst buyer's market - scarce supply, high demand

Risk Assessment:

  • What's the cost of waiting? Will you lose utility/value by delaying?
  • What's the potential savings? How much could you save by waiting?
  • How certain is the timing? Is the sale/price drop guaranteed or speculative?
  • What are your alternatives? Can you use a substitute in the meantime?
Step 6: Develop Strategy

Negotiation Tactics by Market Structure:

Market Type Your Leverage Effective Tactics
Perfect Competition Low - prices are market-set • Shop for convenience/service
• Look for loyalty programs
• Buy in bulk if offered
Oligopoly Moderate - room to negotiate • Mention competitor prices
• Ask for bundle discounts
• Negotiate contract terms
• Visit at end of month/quarter
Monopoly Very low - limited options • Look for promotional periods
• Reduce usage to minimum
• Explore alternatives/substitutes
• Group buying with others

Leverage Points to Emphasize:

  • "I'm ready to buy today" - Immediate sale is valuable
  • "I'm comparing with [competitor]" - Shows you have alternatives
  • "I'm a repeat customer" - Loyalty has value
  • "I can buy multiple units" - Volume discounts
  • "I can pay cash" - Eliminates transaction fees
  • "I'm flexible on features" - Opens up inventory options

Walk-Away Alternatives:

Always have a backup plan:

  • Identify comparable substitutes
  • Know your maximum acceptable price
  • Be prepared to delay the purchase
  • Have another seller ready to contact

The willingness to walk away is your strongest negotiating position.

Quick Reference Checklist

Before Any Major Purchase:

  • ☐ Identified market structure (competition level)
  • ☐ Researched current supply conditions
  • ☐ Analyzed demand patterns and seasonality
  • ☐ Compared prices from 3+ sellers
  • ☐ Checked historical price trends
  • ☐ Determined optimal timing to buy
  • ☐ Identified negotiation leverage points
  • ☐ Developed walk-away alternatives
  • ☐ Set maximum acceptable price
  • ☐ Ready to execute strategy
