Investment Types Reference Sheet

Quick reference guide for stocks, bonds, mutual funds, and ETFs

STOCKS
Ownership shares in a company
Risk Level: High
Historical Return: ~10% annually (S&P 500)
Income: Some pay dividends
Examples: Apple (AAPL), Microsoft (MSFT)
When to Use: Long-term growth goals (10+ years), comfortable with volatility, seeking maximum returns
BONDS
Loans to governments or corporations
Risk Level: Low to Medium
Historical Return: ~3-5% annually
Income: Regular interest payments
Examples: US Treasury, Corporate bonds
When to Use: Seeking stable income, preserving capital, nearing retirement, balancing portfolio risk
MUTUAL FUNDS
Professionally managed portfolios of stocks/bonds
Risk Level: Varies (Medium average)
Historical Return: ~6-8% annually (varies by type)
Fees: 0.5-2% expense ratio
Examples: Vanguard 500 (VFIAX)
When to Use: Want instant diversification, prefer professional management, like simplicity, have $500+ to invest
ETFs (Exchange-Traded Funds)
Baskets of stocks/bonds traded like stocks
Risk Level: Varies (Medium average)
Historical Return: ~8-10% annually (index ETFs)
Fees: 0.03-0.5% expense ratio
Examples: Vanguard S&P 500 (VOO)
When to Use: Want low fees, need flexibility, prefer tax efficiency, comfortable trading during market hours
Key Terms Glossary
Expense Ratio
Annual fee charged by mutual funds/ETFs (e.g., 0.5% = $50 per $10,000 invested)
Dividend
Cash payment companies make to shareholders from profits
Diversification
Spreading investments across many assets to reduce risk
Liquidity
How quickly you can sell an investment and get cash
Index Fund
Fund that tracks a market index (like S&P 500)
Active Management
Manager picks stocks trying to beat the market (higher fees)
Passive Management
Fund tracks an index automatically (lower fees)
Volatility
How much an investment's value fluctuates over time
Investment Selection Decision Tree
→ Do you have less than $500 to invest?
YES: Consider individual stocks or ETFs (can buy fractional shares)
NO: Continue below
→ Is your investment timeline less than 5 years?
YES: Focus on bonds or balanced funds for stability
NO: Continue below
→ Do you want to actively pick individual companies?
YES: Buy individual stocks (higher risk, requires research)
NO: Continue below
→ Do you value low fees over everything else?
YES: Choose low-cost index ETFs (like VOO, VTI)
NO: Continue below
→ Do you prefer automatic investing and simplicity?
YES: Choose mutual funds (especially target-date funds)
NO: Mix of stocks and ETFs based on your preferences
💡 Pro Tip for Beginners: Start with a low-cost S&P 500 index fund or ETF (like VOO or VFIAX). This gives you instant diversification across 500 large U.S. companies with minimal fees. As you learn more, you can expand into bonds, international stocks, and other investments.
